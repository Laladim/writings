There is a verse in Ezekiel 36 that I have been thinking about for a long time.

> I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh.

This was God's promise to a people in exile. They had hardened themselves against Him. They had chased other gods. They had broken the covenant and defiled the land, and the consequences had come. Now, from a distance, the Lord was telling them what He would do when He brought them home. He would not just return them to the land. He would do surgery. He would take the stone out of their chest and put something softer in its place.

I read this passage again on a hard day this week.

If I'm being honest, my heart felt like stone. Not rebellious stone. Just tired stone. Prayers that felt rote. Responses to people I love that came out sharper than I meant them to. Moments where I saw God's goodness in front of me and did not feel moved by it. And so I sat with the verse.

Here is what I did not expect.

I realized I do not get to manufacture a heart of flesh.

I have tried. I have told myself to feel things I did not feel. I have grieved that my affections for the Lord seemed cooler than they should be, and I have spent afternoons trying to warm them up by force. It does not work. You cannot bench-press a heart into softness.

But Ezekiel 36 is not a command. It is a promise.

The verb is God's. He will remove. He will give. The work of softening is not something I do to myself. It is something He does to me. What is mine, what I can actually do, is to come to Him stone-hearted and ask Him to keep His promise.

And so I prayed, small and rough: "Lord, correct the posture of my heart. I do not have the strength to soften myself. Please give me what You said You would give."

The softening was not immediate.

But something did shift, slowly, over the afternoon. A conversation with AJ where I listened instead of defending. A verse that moved me when I would have expected to skim it. A minute of quiet at the kitchen sink where I was just thankful for warm water, and I had not been thankful for anything for a few hours.

It is such a beautiful picture that God is still in the surgery business.

He is not finished with me. He is not finished with any of us. The heart of stone is not the end of the story. The heart of flesh is coming, not because I am strong enough to soften myself, but because the One who promised is faithful.

For the glory of God.

— Lala
