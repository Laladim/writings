Most of my work happens with Claude Code now. I write with it. I plan with it. I debug with it. It has become something like a collaborator that does not get tired.

The problem with a collaborator that does not get tired is that I do.

I used to end my sessions by just closing the laptop. The learning from the day stayed in my head for a few hours and then dissolved. The hard thing I had figured out at 3pm was gone by the next morning. The mistake I had made was usually the mistake I made again a week later.

So I built a small ritual. It is one prompt. It takes three minutes. I use it almost every evening now.

Here is the prompt, word for word:

```
Summarize today's session as a journal entry for future me.
Cover:
1. What was the main thing I worked on and why.
2. One thing I learned (fact, skill, or principle).
3. One mistake I made and the fix.
4. One open question that is still not resolved.
5. What tomorrow's first step should be.
Write in the voice of a kind friend. Keep it under 250 words.
```

That is the whole tool.

A few things about why this has been so helpful to me.

**It turns a session into a letter.** My past self is writing a letter to my future self. That letter has a reader, and the reader is me tomorrow morning. So I do not write for anyone else. I write for the tired Lala who is going to open her laptop at 6am and need to remember where we left off.

**It names the mistake without shame.** Point 3 is the one I almost skipped at first. Naming the mistake felt heavy. But when Claude writes it in the voice of a kind friend, the mistake becomes a gift instead of a weight. The mistake is what taught us something. And the fix is now written down where I can find it.

**It leaves one thread loose.** The open question matters. It keeps tomorrow honest. I will not pretend I solved everything today. The loose thread is an invitation to come back.

**It answers the morning question.** Point 5, the first step for tomorrow, saves me fifteen minutes of staring at the screen. The note tells me what to do. I start.

I have been doing this for a few months now. My session logs are starting to add up to something. A record. A curriculum. A small library of what I have tried and what has worked and what has not, in my own voice. And when I forget something I already knew, I can search the record and find it again.

If you are using AI for real work, I cannot recommend this highly enough. Copy the prompt. Use it tonight. See what it gives back.

And if I'm being honest, the ritual has changed how I think about the day itself. I work differently now, because I know there is a small reckoning coming at the end. That has made me kinder, more intentional, a little less performative.

Which, it turns out, is what most tools are supposed to do. The good ones do not just get work done. They make you a better person.

— Lala
