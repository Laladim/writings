The first time I tried to work from home, my desk was a folding table in the corner of a room I shared with my husband AJ. It wobbled. The edge dug into my wrists when I typed too long. I had one monitor. I had no idea what I was doing.

That was 2010.

I was twenty-something, a new wife, and I believed. I really wanted to believe that God could make a career out of a folding table and a stubborn heart. I did not know yet how long the Lord takes to answer that kind of prayer. I only knew I could not keep working the way I was working, and I did not want to wait for permission from anyone to start.

So I started.

I said yes to work I was not qualified for. I said yes to clients who taught me things by pointing at what I had broken. I learned content by writing too much of it. I learned SEO by getting it wrong. I learned marketing by watching the kind of posts that moved someone and the kind that did not.

And in between all of that, I was sick.

Chronic illness has a way of interrupting a career plan. It did not ask my permission. It took afternoons, then whole days. For a while I thought the illness was the part of my story God would fix before the real story could start. That is not what happened. The story is the story that includes the illness. And somehow, through the folding table and the hospital visits and the long quiet months, the Lord kept teaching me something I could not have learned any other way.

Now, in 2026, I sit at a real desk. Two monitors. A better chair. A microphone that sometimes works and sometimes refuses. Claude Code runs on one screen, a Bible study journal on the other. I am writing this post at that desk.

And the workstation is not the miracle.

The workstation is the evidence of the miracle.

The miracle is that a woman who could barely finish a day at a folding table is still working, fifteen years later, and the work has grown up with her. The Lord was writing a slow story. He was giving me the tools I needed at the pace I could receive them. Folding table, then folding chair, then one monitor, then two, then AI. Each step was a gift I was not ready for until I was.

If you are at a folding table right now, please do not despise the folding table.

It is holy ground.

The Lord is not behind. He is just writing a longer story than we had patience for.

For the glory of God.

— Lala
